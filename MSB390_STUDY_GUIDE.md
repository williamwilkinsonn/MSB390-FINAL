## 1. LEADER HUMILITY

### Etymology & Core Definition
- **Humility** comes from *humus/humai* = "from the ground / on the ground"
- C.S. Lewis: *"Humility is not thinking less of yourself, but thinking of yourself less."*
- Humility = **self-transcendence** (not self-deprecation)
- It is a **temperance virtue** — guards against excess; keeps other characteristics within Aristotle's **Golden Mean** (also: Buddhist "middle way," Confucian *zhong yong*)
- Marianne Williamson: *"Your playing small does not serve the world."*
- Kant: Self-worship (pride) is the **root vice** that spawns all others; humility = tamed, proper, tempered self
- Called the **"mother virtue"** — gives life and nurturance to other virtues
- Solzhenitsyn: The line between good and evil runs **through every human heart** — humility is the portal between the two

### Why It Matters in Business
- The **crisis of leadership** (large-scale corporate scandals) drove academic focus on humility
- Characteristics that get a leader **noticed and promoted** are often the same ones that get them **derailed** — unless tempered by humility
- **Titanic** (arrogance) vs. **Olympic** (humility): same era, very different captains and outcomes

### Stage 1: Self-Awareness (Illumination Stage)
- Like **harrowing soil** — painful but prepares the ground for growth
- Two problematic self-views:

#### Inflated Sense of Self
| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| What it is | Exaggeratedly *positive* view of one's ability AND integrity |
| Key danger | **Conflating exceptional ability with exceptional integrity** |
| Metaphor | Swollen ankle — tender when questioned/challenged |
| Ethical risk | Highest — leads directly to unethical behavior |

**Examples of Inflation:**
- **Andy Fastow** (Enron CFO): CFO of the Year Award → Prison ID card; accolades blinded him to unethicality of mark-to-market accounting / hiding debt
- **Dennis Kozlowski** (Tyco CEO): Borrowed ~$250M from company, didn't hide it — felt track record justified it; served prison time
- **Ashkop** (student): Conflated exceptional academic ability with exceptional integrity
- **Steve Jobs 1.0**: Brilliant but toxic; liquidating value through how he treated people

#### Compressed Sense of Self
| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| What it is | Exaggeratedly *negative* / undersized view of self |
| Key danger | Prevents you from helping others; moral implications when self-compression keeps you from doing good |
| Metaphor | George McFly (*Back to the Future*) — overcame compression when Biff threatened his lady |
| Is it humility? | **NO** — also a form of self-deception and self-fixation |

**Key point:** Both inflation and compression are **self-deception** and **self-fixation** — neither is true humility.

#### Tactics to Overcome Each:
| Problem | Tactic |
|---------|--------|
| **Compression** | **TAGS List** — Talents, Achievements, Goals, Service; ongoing/dynamic; read in the valleys of life |
| **Inflation** | **Personal SWOT Analysis** — must still acknowledge Strengths (self-affirmation), but then face Weaknesses & Threats to ego |

### Stage 2: Teachability
- **Light tubes metaphor**: Grounded in the earth → receptive to electrical energy; when grounded in humility, we receive wisdom from daily experiences
- **Never shed the identity of a student**; write down insights regardless of who is speaking
- Steve Jobs 2.0 = **"Tempered Narcissist"** — still driven/visionary but embraced humility; built Apple into most valuable company in the world
- Steve Jobs 1.0 *could not* have done that; he was "liquidating value" through his behavior

**Xerox PARC Story:**
- Xerox R&D center invented: GUI, mouse, windows-on-screen, networked computers (precursor to email/internet)
- Corporate executives (focused on copy machines) **lacked teachability** → ignored engineers
- Steve Jobs toured PARC and saw the future immediately
- Jobs: "Xerox could have owned the entire computer industry"
- Lesson: **Teachability = market opportunity captured or lost**

**West Point Research:**
- Young major (first day in Afghanistan): resisted the temptation to rely on his West Point designation; said "I need all your help" → resounding success against 3 attacking militant groups including a suicide attack
- Captain (second deployment): went back to retrieve a soldier who couldn't hear over radio fire; wept telling the story; contrast to first deployment where he would have blamed the soldier

**Practical Application:** Leaders who write notes frequently legitimize a **culture of ongoing learning** in their organizations

## 3. COMMODIFICATION OF SOCIETY

### Core Distinction
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Market Economy** | A *tool* for organizing productive activity — legitimate, valuable |
| **Market Society** | A *way of life* where market values seep into every aspect of human endeavor — problematic |

> "Money makes the world go round" = market society thinking

### The Mahan Principle
- The Fall began when Cain killed Abel = **exchanging life for money**
- Don't let money become the measure of all things

### Framework for Evaluating Commodification

**Two Key Questions:**

#### 1. Fairness / Equality
- Does it unfairly **impact** anyone?
- Does it unfairly **coerce** someone?
- Does it interfere with others' **rights**?
- Does it reduce **access** for those the policy was designed to help?

#### 2. Corruption
- Does it **degrade** something, or treat it with a **lower mode of valuation** than it deserves?
- Does it create or destroy **social value**?

### Case Studies

| Case | Key Issue | Verdict |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Disneyland / Line Standing** | Families renting disabled persons to skip lines | Fairness: reduces access for disabled (intended beneficiaries); Corruption: treats disability as commodity |
| **Project Prevention** (Barbara Harris) | Offering $300 to drug-addicted mothers for permanent sterilization | Corruption: treats reproductive capacity as commodity; later amended to temporary contraception |
| **Government Car Decals** | Advertising on police cars | Corruption: compromises impartiality and decorum of the office; still a thriving company |
| **Ford Pinto** | Ford chose not to recall (court costs < fix cost); charged with homicide | Dark side of goal setting; inadequate human capital for critical decisions; **NOT ON EXAM for details** |

### Dark Side of Goal Setting
- Goals create **perverse incentives** when poorly designed
- **Wells Fargo**: Branch quotas for accounts → fake accounts created (no actual cards sent) → massive fallout for a "very reputable" organization
- **Ford**: Recall coordinator vastly overworked; inadequate system to identify design flaws → Ford was the **first company charged with homicide**
- As future managers: goal setting is powerful but requires careful design to avoid perverse incentives

## 4. MAGICAL KINGDOM / GIVING VOICE TO VALUES

### Core Idea (Mary Gentile)
After you've decided what the right thing to do is → prepare **HOW** to say it effectively.

### The Four Voice/Influence Tactics
| Tactic | Description | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Logic and Reason** | Structured, theoretical argument; uses formal rules of logic | When you have time and an analytical audience |
| **Pragmatism** | Real-world precedent, current structures, "this is how it works in practice" | When theoretical arguments won't land; invoke existing laws/norms |
| **Metaphors and Stories** | Narrative approach; emotionally engaging | When relationship management is key; less confrontational |
| **Building Coalitions** | Finding allies; strength in numbers | When you feel isolated; when the issue is systemic |

> **Logic vs. Pragmatism distinction**: Logic can be theoretical (arguing for something that doesn't currently exist). Pragmatism invokes *existing* precedents and structures.

### Key Principles

**Know Your Voice Strengths:**
- Identify your innate influence style before the difficult conversation
- If you're not a confrontational person → lean into metaphors/stories and coalition building

**Beware Direct Dissent (Usually):**
- Try **indirect tactics first**: relationship management, benefit of the doubt, framing in terms of org values
- **Diplomat/team member** vs. **martyr/crusader** — almost always choose diplomat
- "Charging the hill" is a novice ethical response most of the time

**Anticipate Common Dilemma Types:**
- You can't predict specifics, but dilemmas follow predictable structures:
  - Justice vs. Mercy
  - Utilitarianism vs. Deontology
  - Long-term vs. Short-term
  - Honesty vs. Loyalty
- Prepare metaphors and stories for *each type* in advance → "home court advantage"

**Moral Muteness:**
- Organizations where ethical implications are **never voiced**
- Focusing entirely on ROI/revenue → indirect signal that ethics don't matter
- Goal: Avoid moral muteness AND avoid moral superiority; find the middle path

**Framing in Terms of Organizational Values:**
- Says "we're a team" rather than "I'm more moral than you"
- Hard to disagree with: "Let's realign with what we claim to stand for"

| Element | Detail |

| Element | Detail |

### Cross-Film Themes
1. Maintaining moral courage under mounting pressure
2. What happens with repeated exposure to temptation
3. Those with little power often pay the price while executives escape

## 6. SOCIAL FACTORS IN ETHICAL DECISION-MAKING

### Asch Conformity Studies
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Setup** | Subjects asked which line matches the target; confederates give obviously wrong answer |
| **Key Finding** | People conform to group opinion even when objectively wrong |
| **More likely to conform with...** | **Strangers** (more troubling — you don't know their moral history) |
| **Neurological basis** | Non-conformity creates an uncomfortable **error signal** in the brain |
| **Antidote** | **One dissenting voice** dramatically reduces conformity — find ONE ally |
| **Application** | Sharon Watkins (Enron) found Cliff Baxter → together they could whistle-blow |

### Milgram Obedience Studies
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Setup** | Subjects instructed to administer apparently painful shocks to confederates |
| **Purpose** | Understand obedience to authority; what happened in Nazi Germany |
| **Key Quote** | Man who refused: "Oh, I have a lot of choices. My worst choice is not hurting this man." |
| **Lesson** | Something about that man was different — those moral commitments must be driven deep |

### Stanford Prison Experiment (Zimbardo)
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Setup** | Stanford students assigned roles of prisoners and guards |
| **Purpose** | Test **role binding** — does role overwhelm personality? |
| **Key Finding** | Role binding is **far more powerful than expected**; untrained boys acted brutally in days |
| **Role Binding** | Being placed in a role activates scripts and schemas that can overwhelm moral commitments and personality |
| **Reassurance** | In EVERY iteration, some people refused to conform — we **always have a choice** |
| **Application** | Personal code of ethics prepares you to resist role binding effects |

### Moral Courage — Three Required Elements
1. **Commitment to moral principles**
2. **Risk / danger / sacrifice** present in the situation (without this, courage is irrelevant)
3. **Willingness to endure** — moral courage is usually a *process* over time, not a single heroic moment (Sharon Watkins, WorldCom/Tyco whistleblowers endured for months)

### Methods of Dissent

| Type | When to Use | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Direct Dissent** | Time-critical; someone about to be hurt; no other option | Morton Thiokol engineer saying NO to NASA on Challenger launch night |
| **Indirect Dissent** | Default first approach; relationship management | Appeals for more info; framing in org values; seeking third alternative |

**Indirect Dissent Methods:**
- Ask for more information
- Frame in terms of organizational values (Ethics Academy Awards strategy)
- Seek a creative **third alternative**
- Build a coalition

### Enron — Social Factors
- **Mark-to-market accounting**: Unusual method of assessing assets; enabled hiding of debt
- **Sharon Watkins**: Hired mid-scandal; found Cliff Baxter as one ally → blew the whistle together
- Cultural enablers: "Posted the stock price in the elevator"; "If I had to step on another's throat to get ahead, I'd do it"

## 7. STAKEHOLDER THEORY

### Core Definition
> A **stakeholder** is a person with an interest or concern in something, especially a business.

### Edward Freeman (Pioneer)
- "When all stakeholders row in the same direction → **swing** → exponential power and competitive advantage"
- Analogy: Focusing only on shareholders = saying "red blood cells are life" — you need them, but you need so much more

### Primary vs. Secondary Stakeholders (Early Theory)
| Type | Relationship | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Primary** | Formal/contractual; clear enforceable duties | Shareholders, employees, customers, suppliers, creditors, boards, retailers |
| **Secondary** | Informal/implicit; nebulous claims | Community groups, environment, regulators, activists |

**Problem with this model:** Too broad; corporations felt overwhelmed → pendulum swung to "only maximize shareholder value" → stakeholder theory fell out of favor

### ⭐ Stakeholder Salience Model (Mitchell, Agle & Wood) — EXAM CRITICAL

**Three Criteria:**
| Criterion | Definition |
|-----------|------------|
| **Power** | Ability to get the company to do something it otherwise wouldn't do |
| **Legitimacy** | A commonly agreed-upon **moral** claim; a moral connection between the stakeholder's needs and the company's actions |
| **Urgency** | Calling for immediate attention; currently demanding action (latent stakeholders = less salient) |

**Venn Diagram Categories — MEMORIZE:**

```
                    POWER
                   /     \
              Dormant   Dangerous
                /           \
          DEFINITIVE (all 3)
                \           /
           Dependent   Dominant
                   \     /
                 Legitimacy + Urgency
                 
Non-Stakeholder: NONE of the three
```

| # Criteria | Category | Which Criteria |
|-----------|----------|----------------|
| **3/3** | **Definitive** ⭐ | Power + Legitimacy + Urgency — TOP PRIORITY |
| **2/3** | **Dominant** | Power + Legitimacy |
| **2/3** | **Dangerous** | Power + Urgency |
| **2/3** | **Dependent** | Legitimacy + Urgency |
| **1/3** | **Dormant** | Power only |
| **1/3** | **Discretionary** | Legitimacy only |
| **1/3** | **Demanding** | Urgency only |
| **0/3** | **Non-Stakeholder** | None — no obligation |

**Key Distinction from Previous Model:** Previous model couldn't tell you who's IN and who's OUT. Salience model gives you a clear boundary.

**Practical Uses:**
- *Inside* org: Prioritize agendas based on who has all three
- *Outside* org: Understand what you need to gain to become a definitive stakeholder (e.g., employees unionizing to gain power → move from Dependent to Definitive)

### Case Studies

**Daughter with Wallet Example:**
- Demanding allowance: **Demanding stakeholder** (urgency, no power, no legitimacy)
- Pulls out a knife: Upgrades to **Dangerous stakeholder** (power + urgency, still no legitimacy)

**Employee Wanting a Raise:**
- No raise for 5 years, asking boss → **Dependent** (legitimacy + urgency, no power)
- Workers unionize → gain power → becomes **Definitive**

**Costco Case:**
- Chose to "overpay" and over-insure employees vs. industry benchmarks
- Wall Street complained → CEO Jim Sinegal: *"In order to reward shareholders long-term, you have to please your customers and take care of your workers"*
- Results: Annual turnover **half of Walmart**; annual sales **almost double Sam's Club**; spends **$0 on advertising**
- Lesson: Strategically prioritizing a non-obvious stakeholder can create massive competitive advantage

**MS Drug Pharmaceutical Case:**
- Miracle drug for MS; FDA fast-tracked; only 20,000 units in year 1 (demand: 100,000)
- Pricing decision: $50K/year (supply shortage pricing) vs. $10K/year (planned price)
- Allocation: Market decides (first-come, first-served) vs. company controls (prioritize early-stage patients)
- Lesson: Identifying your definitive stakeholders shapes both ethical and strategic decisions

### Milton Friedman Critique
- Companies are **stewards of shareholder money** — taking on CSR = making charitable decisions without consent
- **Tommy/Pizza analogy**: Team gives you money for pizza; Tommy donates it to a homeless veteran → he used someone else's money without their consent
- Friedman: Return dividends; let shareholders choose their own charities
- **His argument has merit** — but CSR can have a strong business case too

### Pros & Cons of Stakeholder Theory
| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Induces companies to shoulder externalities | Makes quick decision-making harder |
| Encourages long-term thinking | Creates an overwhelming list of obligations |
| Promotes CSR | Can pressure companies into wasteful charitable platitudes |
| Better organizational health & sustainability | May dilute focus on core business |

## 8. BYU ADMISSIONS & CONFLICT OF INTEREST

### Conflict of Interest

**Definition:** A situation where multiple roles could compromise your **objectivity** in making the best decision on behalf of someone else.

**Core principle:** Conflicts of interest are **increasingly inevitable** as you grow in career — having one is NOT bad. Having an **undisclosed** one IS.

**The Protocol:**
1. **Disclose** — always the first step; knee-jerk reaction when you perceive a potential conflict
2. **Withdraw** if necessary — even if the other party says it's OK (professor example: editor approved, professor still withdrew)
3. Manage the **perception** of conflict of interest — not just the actual conflict (TA example: announced to class)

**Reciprocity Rule (Psychology):**
- Lavish gifts create a psychological obligation to reciprocate → compromises objectivity
- Even if gifts feel normalized in an industry, be careful

**Key Cases:**
| Scenario | Resolution |
|----------|------------|
| Vendor offering a golf trip to Honolulu to a key decision-maker | Disclose; consult company policy; be very careful |
| Hiring your cousin (equivalent qualifications) | Disclose; let others make final decision; share what you uniquely know about character |
| TA grading a classmate they're dating | Another TA grades all their work; announce the arrangement to the class |
| Reviewing an academic paper by grant co-authors | Disclose; then withdraw anyway despite editor's approval |
| Chaplain told a soldier plans to attempt suicide | "What are the principles you can live with long-term?" |

**Gift Policy:**
- Learn your organization's gift policy **immediately**
- Federal government: often **$0** — not even a fancy pen
- Brad Agle example: Received $1,000 of premium steaks → held a company barbecue (best transparent solution)

---

### BYU Admissions Case — Affirmative Action & Diversity

**True or False: Affirmative action is unconstitutional?**
**→ FALSE (generally)** — the Supreme Court allows a constrained version

#### In Education:
- Race **CANNOT** be converted to a numerical score/quota (University of Michigan struck down; Harvard struck down)
- Race **CAN** be considered case-by-case if an applicant explains in essays how their racial background gave them experiences adding value to the university community
- Banned as a direct factor in admissions decisions

#### In Employment (most states):
Affirmative action plans are legal IF they meet ALL conditions:
1. **Written down** / formalized
2. **Prove a historic disparity** exists (underrepresented minority or women in traditionally segregated job categories)
3. **Narrowly tailored** — not broader than necessary
4. **Temporary** — must end once disparity is remedied
5. **Does not create reverse discrimination** — cannot unduly harm non-beneficiaries

**Exception:** Affirmative action in employment is **NOT legal** in California, Michigan, and Washington.

**BYU Strategy:** Increasing diversity by going **internationally** for applicants — legitimate, avoids constitutional issues

**Why Diversity Matters:** Adds creativity, more nuanced perspectives, specialization, and reduces groupthink

## 9. ETHICS ACADEMY AWARDS

### Purpose
Capstone experience — apply multiple course concepts to prepare for a **high-stakes ethical conversation** with your manager.

### Moral Muteness
- **Definition:** Organizational culture where ethical implications of decisions are **never discussed** — everyone talks ROI, revenue, strategy, but never ethics
- Creates an indirect signal that ethics don't matter
- Leads to legally/morally risky decisions going unchallenged
- **Goal:** Avoid moral muteness — but also avoid moral superiority

### High-Stakes Conversations — Preparation Framework
1. **Identify the moral principles** in tension
2. **Know your voice strengths** (which of the 4 tactics: logic/reason, pragmatism, metaphors/stories, coalitions)
3. **Frame in terms of organizational values** — cast yourself as a team player, not a crusader
4. **Seek the creative third alternative** — binary thinking is lazy ethical thinking
5. **Diplomat/team member** role → rarely the martyr/crusader
6. **Anticipate common dilemma structures** (justice vs. mercy, etc.) and prepare in advance

### The 13 Dilemma Types (Halverson / Business Ethics Field Guide)
Common structural tensions:
- Truth vs. Loyalty
- Individual vs. Community
- Short-term vs. Long-term
- Justice vs. Mercy
- Standing up to authority / Power
- Utilitarianism vs. Deontology
- Honesty vs. Self-interest
- And others in the Field Guide

**For each type:** Know what moral principles are in tension, what metaphors/stories apply, what the pragmatic argument looks like

## 10. CORPORATE SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY (CSR)

### Definition (Seldon)
> Companies **voluntarily** contributing to a better society, producing an overall positive impact that **exceeds** legal, ethical, and public expectations.

### Three Types of CSR
1. **Humane employee practices**
2. **Caring for the environment**
3. **Philanthropic endeavors**

### Key Statistic
- **70 of the top 100 world economies are corporations** (not countries) — enormous power and corresponding responsibility

### Internal Reasons for CSR
| Reason | Example |
|--------|---------|
| **Leader Imprinting / Cascading Leadership Theory**: Founder's values cascade throughout the org | HP — Hewlett & Packard's founding documents included CSR before it was trendy |
| **Enhanced employee identification/morale** | Medtronic — brought in pacemaker recipients; one did backflips across the stage; morale boosted for a year |
| **Recruit and retain mission-driven talent** | Employees more committed and motivated when proud of their company |

### External Reasons for CSR
| Reason | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Stakeholder theory** | Acknowledge and address externalities / secondary stakeholder liabilities before they become lawsuits |
| **Corporate rebranding** | Combat perception of corporations as heartless/elitist/money-hungry |
| **Public trust** | Study: fewer than 1 in 5 people trust business leaders; same study found people trust *politicians* MORE than business leaders |

### ⭐ CSR Effectiveness Framework — The Key to Effective CSR

```
         CAPABILITIES           LIABILITIES
       (What you do best)   (Harms your biz creates)
              +                      +
         BOTH together = EFFECTIVE CSR
```

**Capability:** Leverage your core competencies — what can your org do that no one else can?

**Liability:** What harms or externalities does your business create? Take ownership.

**Effective CSR does BOTH.**

### Case Studies

**Coca-Cola (India/Pakistan Commercial):**
- ✅ Leveraged capability: World-class marketing bringing people together
- ❌ Ignored liability: Coca-Cola plants in rural Pakistan depleting aquifers; farmers' crops failing; generational farmers committing suicide
- Under pressure, eventually relocated plants
- Lesson: Great CSR leverages competency AND addresses liability

**HP:**
- Bill Hewlett & Dave Packard: CSR embedded in founding documents since the Palo Alto garage
- Thought leader in CSR long before it was trendy

**Medtronic:**
- CSR = connecting employees to purpose
- Brought in patients whose lives were saved by pacemakers → one did backflips across stage
- Morale boost lasted a year

**Joplin, Missouri Tornado:**
- **Duracell**: Charging stations + thousands of batteries
- **Tide**: Semi-trucks with washing machines and dryers
- **Verizon**: Free WiFi for all
- **Little Caesars**: 20,000 slices of pizza
- Lesson: Each company leveraged their **specific competency** in disaster relief

**Ineffective CSR (Anti-Example):**
- Executives under peer pressure hand $5M to a VP: "Go do some tree-hugging stuff"
- VP calls uncle who runs an inefficient nonprofit and wires him the money
- **Wasteful charitable platitude** — no capability, no liability addressed

**Goats in Ghana (Anti-Example):**
- Well-intentioned: Give goats to villagers for food security
- Disrupted fragile social ecologies; recipients ostracized; goats killed out of envy
- Lesson: Fragile economies and social ecologies are often far more complex than outsiders expect; good intentions ≠ good outcomes

## 11. BUSINESS & GOVERNMENT / ENRON

### Ethical Leadership (Brown & Trevino, 2006)
- Leader demonstrates normatively appropriate conduct
- **Promotes and fosters** ethical conduct in followers
- Based on **Social Learning Theory**: Leaders must be socially attractive AND credible role models
- "Attractiveness" goes beyond authority and status — people must *want* to emulate you

### Why Leaders Lose Their Way (Bill George)
> *"Leaders who lose their way are not necessarily bad people; they lose their moral bearings, often yielding to seductions in their paths."*
> *"Very few people go into leadership roles to cheat or do evil."*

- They are seduced; moral compass drifts; circumstances compound

### Enron — The Most Classic Contemporary Example of Absent Ethical Leadership

**Factors:**
| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Leadership** | Extreme culture; "Posted stock price in elevator"; "If I had to step on another's throat to get ahead, I'd do it" |
| **Culture** | "Bad greed doesn't go very far unless structurally reinforced" — structure enabled corruption |
| **Accomplices** | Banks and auditing firms (Arthur Andersen) |
| **Accounting** | **Mark-to-market accounting** — unusual method; enabled hiding debt; Sharon Watkins called it "indefensible" |
| **Andy Fastow** | CFO of Year → Prison ID; external validation → inflated sense of self → moral blindness |
| **Sharon Watkins** | Found Cliff Baxter as one ally (social conformity antidote!) → blew the whistle |
| **Result** | **Sarbanes-Oxley Act** catalyzed |
| **Legacy** | Harvard gave Enron the "Ig Nobel Prize for Most Creative Use of Imaginary Numbers" |

### Challenger Disaster (Morton Thiokol / NASA)
- Engineers knew O-rings would freeze in cold temperatures the morning of the launch
- Management pressure to maintain NASA contracts overrode engineering concerns
- Classic case where **direct dissent was the right call** — lives at stake, time-critical
- Example of role binding and authority pressure producing tragic outcomes

### Jared Halverson — "Proving Contraries"
- Common moral principles frequently come into **tension** (not just one principle — always competing values)
- Understanding these tensions in advance = preparation for ethical conversations
- Related to Gentile's work on anticipating dilemma types

### 13 Dilemma Types (Business Ethics Field Guide)
- Keep this sheet WITH YOU during the exam
- Common types include:
  - Truth vs. Loyalty
  - Individual vs. Community
  - Short-term vs. Long-term
  - Justice vs. Mercy
  - Standing up to authority
  - Deontological vs. Utilitarian obligations
- **For each type**: Know the structure, typical stakeholders, and possible value-proposition scripts

- Keep this sheet WITH YOU during the exam

- Study the specific techniques listed in the Field Guide and their **counter-statements**

## 13. OPEN-ENDED ESSAY PREP

There are **4 open-ended questions** (short — not even half a page each). Likely cover major frameworks. Suggested preparation for each:

### Essay Type 1: Apply Humility Framework
**Prompt style:** "Describe how each of the three dimensions of humility is relevant to ethical decision-making, using examples."

**Answer Structure:**
1. Stage 1 (Self-Awareness): Inflated self → Fastow/Enron; compressed self → McFly; both = self-deception
2. Stage 2 (Teachability): Grounded → receptive; Xerox PARC failure; West Point success
3. Stage 3 (Appreciation of Others): Macro/micro mentors; transcend competitive lens
4. Close: A humble leader is more ethical because they are more accurate, more open to feedback, and more focused on others than self-promotion

---

### Essay Type 2: Apply Stakeholder Salience Model
**Prompt style:** "Given a scenario, identify and categorize the relevant stakeholders using the Mitchell, Agle & Wood model."

**Answer Structure:**
1. Define the three criteria (power, legitimacy, urgency)
2. Go through each stakeholder → assign which criteria they have → assign category
3. Recommend priority order based on categories
4. Note: Non-stakeholders need not be addressed

---

### Essay Type 3: Apply Giving Voice to Values
**Prompt style:** "You discover your manager is asking you to do something unethical. Using Gentile's framework, explain how you would prepare and respond."

**Answer Structure:**
1. Identify which of the 4 voice tactics fits your strengths
2. Identify which dilemma type this is (justice vs. mercy? honesty vs. loyalty?)
3. Frame response in terms of **organizational values** (diplomat, not martyr)
4. Start with indirect descent; escalate to direct only if necessary
5. Prepare your specific argument: metaphor/story OR pragmatic precedent OR logical structure
6. Anticipate pushback and prepare responses

---

### Essay Type 4: Apply CSR Effectiveness Framework
**Prompt style:** "Describe an effective CSR strategy for a company of your choice."

**Answer Structure:**
1. Identify company's **core competencies** (what they uniquely do well)
2. Identify company's **liabilities** (what harms their business creates)
3. Design 2-3 CSR initiatives that leverage competency AND address liability
4. Briefly note: Internal benefits (employee morale) + external benefits (stakeholder management, rebranding)
5. Contrast with ineffective CSR (wasteful platitudes that do neither)

### Stakeholder Salience at a Glance
```
All 3 (P+L+U) → Definitive ⭐
P+L           → Dominant
P+U           → Dangerous ⚠️
L+U           → Dependent
P only        → Dormant
L only        → Discretionary
U only        → Demanding
None          → Non-Stakeholder ❌
```

### Commodification Test
```
Ask: 1. Fairness — does it unfairly coerce/impact/reduce access?
     2. Corruption — does it treat something with lower valuation than deserved?
```

### CSR Effectiveness
```
Good CSR = Capability (leverage what you do best)
         + Liability (address harms you create)
Bad CSR  = Wasteful charitable platitudes (neither)
```

### Conflict of Interest Protocol
```
1. Disclose immediately
2. Withdraw if necessary (even if told it's OK)
3. Manage the PERCEPTION, not just the actual conflict
```

### Giving Voice to Values — 4 Tactics
```
1. Logic/Reason     (theoretical, structured)
2. Pragmatism       (real-world precedent, "this is how it works")
3. Metaphors/Stories (narrative, relational)
4. Build Coalitions  (allies, strength in numbers)
→ Default to indirect dissent; direct only when time-critical/lives at stake
```
